Frequently Asked Questions

What is a fair line?
A fair line (or fair odds) is the theoretical price at which neither side of a bet has an edge — the true implied probability converted to American odds with no bookmaker margin. FairLine computes this from its model probability.
What does edge mean?
Edge is the difference between the model's estimated win probability and the market's implied probability (after removing the vig). A positive edge means the model believes the bet is priced better than the market suggests.
What is no-vig market probability?
Bookmakers add a margin (vig or juice) to odds so both sides sum to more than 100%. No-vig probability removes that margin, giving the market's true implied probability for each side. FairLine uses Pinnacle's no-vig line as the market benchmark.
Why compare to the no-vig market?
Pinnacle accepts sharp money and typically offers the most efficient lines in the market. Beating Pinnacle's no-vig line consistently is the gold standard for demonstrating model edge. It's a much higher bar than beating soft-book lines.
What is closing-line value (CLV)?
CLV measures how the odds at bet time compared to the final closing line before the game. Positive CLV means bets were placed at better prices than the market settled on — a strong signal that the model is identifying genuine edge rather than getting lucky results.
What is Kelly sizing?
Kelly Criterion is a formula that sizes bets proportional to your edge and odds. FairLine uses Half-Kelly (50% of full Kelly) to reduce variance while still betting larger on higher-edge plays. Stake sizes are shown as a percentage of a 10-unit bankroll.
Are these guaranteed picks?
No. Sports betting always involves risk and variance. The model identifies positive expected value based on historical patterns and market signals — not certainty. Never bet more than you can afford to lose.
How are live results different from backtests?
Live results are picks posted on the site before first pitch and tracked in real time. Backtests are historical simulations on past data and are inherently subject to overfitting. FairLine keeps these clearly separated — live performance is the only real validation.
